# Changes made since Part II

### STRUCTURE:
My first structure attempted to follow what was outlined in my first storyboard. While some users like the flow, others were left confused. I realized that the overall flow of the presentation was jumpy. It went as follows: story of financial abuse, retirement concern for stay-at-home moms, benefits of stay-at-home parenting, financial insecurity, retirement concerns, financial abuse, resources. I wanted to adjust this to make the flow of the story more cohesive and with less jumps. While I kept the beginning story, I made clearer transitions and reordered sections. The final structure is a story of financial abuse, benefits of stay-at-home parents, concerns of this groups financial security, concerns of stay-at-home moms’ retirement security, vulnerability and definition of financial abuse, and then resources. I believe this flow solves previous problems and will help readers understand my argument better. 

### Hook with a Story
My presentation begins a story to hook users while also demonstrating a realistic example of what financial and domestic abuse can look like. Definitions are not salient, and people are overconfident in their ability to not end up in a DV situation. By using a story, I help to combat this.
User interviews and feedback informed me that my original story was too long. I shortened the beginning story to highlight the aspects users liked and took out what they identified as fluff. I also changed the slide following the story to highlight that this is a real story and how this problem concerns everyone, not just women currently in abusive situations. I do not cite the story as it has no citation, it is a story from someone close to me and I do not want to disclose them. I do have their consent to use it in the manner I did, however. 

### Visualizing Financial Security
I decided to use bar charts to allow for easy comparison between different groups’ financial security. I approximated financial security by peoples’ responses to the question “Have you set aside emergency or rainy day funds that would cover your expenses for 3 months in case of sickness, job loss, economic downturn, or other emergencies?.” If someone said yes, it can be assumed they have a basic level of financial security. If not, they are not likely financially secure. I then compared average responses (1 – yes) between men and women, mothers, stay-at-home-moms, and childless women, and between different parent types. I followed gender-stereotypical colors to represent men (blue) and women (pink); I kept the same colors for each chart to ensure users did not have to relearn what each color represented. 
Based on user feedback, I made the following adjustments. I renamed the variable “Childcare as a reason not to work” to “Stay-at-Home Parent” so people did not have to interpret what I meant. My initial variable title was kept to best reflect the survey question and not mislead users, though I recognize that it is justified to adjust the title to reflect what it represents. I adjusted these Tableau visualizations to embedded links rather than screenshots. The original embedding included a colossal amount of white space at the bottom of each visualization, so I adjusted each chart to ‘fit height.’ However, this left my charts larger than my desktop screen, making them less than useful. I could not solve this issue and opted for the large amount of white space rather than the too-large charts. I wish shorthand or tableau had any intuitive means to manually adjust the size of what I include in them, but they do not. I considered returning the visualizations to screenshots; however, the Tableau link allows users to hover over each bar and see the exact average responses. 

### Retirement Savings Comparison Visualization
I wanted to include a visual representation of how little mothers have saved for retirement compared to recommended amounts. First, I attempted to accomplish this in Tableau using either a bar chart or tree-map. Neither achieved the final product I wanted. I turned to a digital platform, Canva, to manually create the visualization. I wanted a square which represented the amount of money recommended, then a smaller proportional square to show the amount of money women had saved. Originally, I based the height and width on the ratio between the desired and actual values, which made them disproportional. I adjusted the visually-scaled box graphic to scale on total pixels, or their area, instead. The first box represents $451,400, while the second box represents $100,000. $100,000/$451400 is 0.22, which is the ratio I used to scale the areas of each box. The first square’s area is 25 inches squared; with this ratio the second box’s area is 5.5 inches squared. I also adjusted the boxes to be iterative and fit within the first box to make it easier to see how little mothers have saved compared to what is recommended. I used green to represent each amount, with more of the box becoming white with each iterative section. For the final section, which represents how 80% of mothers aged 50-64 have less than $100,000 saved, I used a gradient scale of green to emphasize that mothers in this group have less than the boxes’ size equivalent of money. 

### Sources
I also ensured to have citations for everything I used. I ended up using an APA in-text citation format with a bibliography at the end. I hyperlinked each in-text citation so that users can access them easily.  

### Call-to-Action
I created three groups of potential users to address their different call-to-actions. For the first, those not yet in a relationship or family planning, I provided links to explanations on different savings and investments. For those in a relationship and planning to have a stay-at-home parent, I included a link to information on Spousal IRAs. For those in abusive relationships, I included a hotline, a guide, escape plan guide, and other support services which may be of use. I wanted every type of user to be able to take something from this presentation, either in building their own financial security, in setting intention in one’s relationships, or knowing where help lies for DV situations. 


## The audience

I touch on this in my call to action, but this presentation could serve most anyone. I identified three specific groups: those not yet in a relationship or family planning, those in a relationship which may include a stay-at-home parent, and those who may be in an abusive relationship. I did not narrow my focus; rather, I categorized it. In conversations about my presentation, most people understood that, while the focus was on stay-at-home mothers, they also could gain from it in their point in their own lives. The only specific adjustment I made to address the audience was the final section, in which I directly address each group and provide advice or sources for further information.

# Final thoughts

Overall, I am pretty happy with how this project turned out. I was happy to be able to use a new system to create a data visualization. I wish that the implementation of the Tableau visualizations went better than reality. If I had more time, I would figure out how to remove the white space from underneath each bar chart. I think I told the story I wanted to tell, though if I could re-do this presentation, I may have chosen a story in which I wanted more Tableau-based visualizations to get more practice. I could have made more visualizations based on the survey data I had; however, I would have been adding them just to add them. Additionally, while I did not use AI for this final part, I am disappointed in how the tool did not help me with Tableau in previous iterations. Nothing in my presentation at the end was AI-generated, nor supported by AI. Finally, as a minor note, each of the turnitin flagged portions were based on my citations or cited quotes.
